Work, Life, Laugh & Love | 168

This survey takes an honest look at where your time and energy really go across 16 areas of life. In six parts, you will map your week, chart the results, decide what to change, and leave with an action plan. The exercise is based on Rainer Strack's Strategic Life Portfolio method. As you answer, think of an average week over the past 12 months rather than just last week.

Before you begin

What does a great life mean to you?

Our goals and objectives change as we age. Some of us plan to attend college, start a family, or launch a new business. Others focus on financial security and early retirement. Some seek a relaxed family life or greater peace and tranquility. And some of us dream of owning big, shiny toys.

Failing to plan is planning for failure in business and in life. If we prepare our minds and bodies for a task, even a small task, we can achieve it because we practice direction. Direction becomes experience. In the bible, so as we think, so to do we become.

This exercise helps students of every age to examine personal priorities, life goals, and time allocation across 16 strategic life factors. We can shift our priorities in bad economic weather or personal tragedy. In the end, life happens. Plan. Be prepared.

    Part 4

    See your life portfolio

    With your Part 3 numbers confirmed, here's your whole life on a single chart.

    You can change your ratings and the graph changes. The farther right a bubble appears on the X-axis, the greater your SATISFACTION. The higher it appears on the Y-axis, the greater its IMPORTANCE. The size of each bubble represents the number of hours you spend on that activity. The upper left quadrant is where factors matter the most, but aren't getting the attention they deserve.

    Rate at least one area in Part 2 (importance, satisfaction, and hours) and your portfolio appears right here.
    We draw the dividing line at 5 by default. Slide it if you like. Your choice is saved with your results.

    The numbers behind the chart

    Three quick calculations to point you at what matters most. Pressure is importance × (10 minus satisfaction). The higher the score, the more that area is asking for your attention. Target hours shows what your week would look like if time simply followed importance (a compass, not a schedule). Time gap is target minus current: positive means an area may be starved for time; negative means there may be hours to win back.
